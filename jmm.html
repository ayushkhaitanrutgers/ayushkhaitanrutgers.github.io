Panel on the Use of AI Tools for Mathematics Research

Exploring how mathematicians are embracing the latest innovations in AI, and how computer scientists are building tools to support research breakthroughs.

The JMM panel, titled “The use of AI tools in Mathematics research,” was organized by Swarat Chaudhuri, Ayush Khaitan and Amitayush Thakur. It brought together leading mathematicians and computer scientists working in the field of AI and mathematics.

The motivation for the panel was two-fold: (1) to let these experts describe the current state of AI adoption in mathematics research, and (2) for the computer science community to gain a deeper understanding of the tools that would be most useful for mathematicians.

Given below are some highlights from our panel discussion. Any factual errors are the author’s alone.

Panelists

  • Francois Charton (FAIR, Meta)
  • Thomas Hubert (Google DeepMind)
  • Alex Kontorovich (Rutgers University)
  • Kaiyu Yang (FAIR, Meta)

Before the discussion began, the moderator welcomed the panelists and explained the structure of the panel. The moderator would ask questions either to a particular panelist or to all panelists collectively. The panelists were free to answer both types of questions, and they were encouraged to share technical insights from their own research to provide context.

On the current use of AI tools in Mathematics Research

The discussion began by asking all the panelists about the kinds of AI tools currently being utilized in mathematics research. Alex highlighted that foundational large language models (LLMs) like ChatGPT and Claude are widely used by mathematicians. He likened discussing research ideas with a powerful LLM to chatting with an expert during tea time in a math department.

Alex also mentioned that tools such as Lean are slowly gaining traction, along with FunSearch, AlphaGeometry, and PatternBoost. He noted that while DeepMind is developing the Alpha family of technologies, access to these tools by mathematics professors could significantly help their research programs. Thomas agreed that open access to similar technologies would be helpful to mathematicians at universities.

Francois Charton then shared his experience from a semester-long workshop at Harvard CMSA, where he assisted in solving complicated math problems using basic Transformers. He emphasized that Transformers can grasp complex mathematical concepts and provide solutions that might be challenging even for professional mathematicians.

On Funsearch and PatternBoost

Moving on to the application of FunSearch and PatternBoost, the panel discussed their use in constructing examples and counterexamples in combinatorics. Francois noted that combinatorics is often more amenable to such methods due to finite search spaces. However, extending these methods to fields like differential geometry or analysis poses challenges.

The moderator suggested that even in geometry, where there might be a finite list of manifolds to consider, automation using LLMs coupled with FunSearch or PatternBoost could be feasible. Francois agreed, proposing that geometry problems might be tackled by dividing manifolds into finite components, a possible avenue for future work.

On AlphaProof and the IMO

The conversation then shifted to AlphaProof, an AI tool that is “almost” at the proficiency of a gold medalist at the International Mathematical Olympiad (IMO). Thomas expressed that grounding LLMs with formal theorem provers like Lean is expected to bring major progress to research-level problem solving.

He clarified that while AlphaProof had been trained on IMO problems, it was trained in a manner resembling a human studying for these competitions, rather than simply memorizing large datasets of solutions. This approach, he said, holds promise for tackling research-level math problems.

Kaiyu Yang elaborated on AlphaProof’s performance, noting it solved 4 out of 6 problems at the 2024 IMO. The two unsolved combinatorics problems involved non-standard words like “monsters,” which complicated translation into Lean. Kaiyu posited that a more standard phrasing of the questions might have allowed AlphaProof to do even better, and that the integration of LLMs with formal theorem provers holds promise for handling the toughest problems in mathematics.

Do LLMs actually understand mathematics?

Addressing the skepticism around LLMs’ understanding of mathematical concepts, Francois Charton argued that mathematical formulas can be encoded as sentences. In his Harvard CMSA research, he demonstrated that transformers could translate mathematical questions and formulas into answers—much like translating English into French.

Meanwhile, Alex Kontorovich compared ChatGPT’s ability to play chess merely by “reading” about the game, raising doubts about whether LLMs have deeper understanding. Francois maintained, however, that transformers can reliably solve most mathematical problems and logical puzzles on which they are adequately trained or fine-tuned.

Conclusion

The panel concluded with an electric atmosphere, as the experts wove intuitive explanations and technical details into their answers for the ~100 mathematicians and computer scientists in attendance.

By the end, there was a palpable feeling that something fundamental has changed about the nature of mathematics research—and that this certainly won’t be the last discussion panel on AI tools in math.
